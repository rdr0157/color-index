Good morning, Thank you for your email. I wanted to provide some clarification regarding the erosion hazard mapping referenced in your message. There are several critical area layers available through the County’s public GIS portal. The layer you cited appears to be derived from soil classification data and is also referenced as CAO Map CA-4D for this area. In future reviews, I would recommend comparing all available critical area datasets, including the County’s slope percent layer, also referenced as CAO Map CA-4C. While the slope percent layer is not the highest-resolution dataset available, it generally provides a more accurate representation of mapped slope conditions than the soil-based erosion hazard layer. That said, neither of those public GIS layers is of sufficient resolution to make a final, site-specific critical area designation. Please see the attached map, which I prepared by georeferencing your submitted site plan. The slope information shown on the attached map is derived from USGS 3DEP data from 2023 and DNR LiDAR data from 2022–2023. Both datasets indicate slopes greater than 15 percent within portions of the project area. I have also included links and references to those datasets for your review. As noted in WWCC 18.08.510(B), these maps are used as guides for the County, project applicants, and property owners, and may be updated as new information becomes available. They are intended as references and do not provide a final critical area designation. In this case, the public GIS layer cited in your email does not provide sufficient detail for the site-specific review of the proposed quarry. The higher-resolution DEM data referenced above is more consistent with the conditions I observed during my site visit. Based on the available information, a critical areas permit and associated report is still required for the proposed activity. Please let me know if you have any questions.
